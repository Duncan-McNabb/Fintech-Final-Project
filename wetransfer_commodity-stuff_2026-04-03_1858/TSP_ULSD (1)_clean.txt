Introduction
What is ULSD?
Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel (ULSD) is a middle distillate fuel refined from crude oil with sulphur content capped at 15 ppm. ULSD became U.S. and Canadian standards in the mid-2000s in an effort to shift to a fuel source that was more environmentally friendly and compatible with emerging technologies. As of right now, you can only trade ULSD futures/options on the NYMEX exchange, and it is used as a benchmark for diesel pricing in North America.

Importance of ULSD
ULSD matters because it is:
The primary fuel source for heavy transport like trucks, boats, and rail. These vehicles help transport nearly all consumable products.
Powers essential industries such as construction and agriculture.
Doubles as heating oil particularly in the Northeast U.S. during the winter, as a substitute to natural gas.
Used to fuel backup generators in large buildings, and is a primary source of electricity in very remote areas.

ULSD Production Centers
There are five Petroleum Administration for Defense Districts (PADDs) in the United States that organize refining and product supply, but ULSD production is mostly concentrated in three zones: PADD 3, PADD 2, and PADD 5. The Gulf Coast (PADD 3) is the largest refining hub in the country, representing approximately half of the refining capacity in the United States. The West Coast (PADD 5) operates as a more isolated market which is reliant on regional crude and imports.
Globally, diesel production is primarily centered in regions with large and complex refineries. The U.S. Gulf Coast, Europe, and Asia (especially China, India, and Singapore) are all major players in diesel production, supplying both domestic markets and export flows. Europe typically relies on imports from the U.S. or Middle East, while the U.S. Gulf Coast and Asia are major exporters.

Location Spreads
USGC: Gulf coast diesel often trades at a discount due to excess supply as this is the main refining hub for ULSD.
NYH: The Colonial pipeline (100mgpd) brings refined products from the gulf coast to the NY Harbor area where the benchmark price is set for the east coast.
West Coast: This market typically trades at a premium due to its more isolated network of pipelines, strict California regulations. Relies heavily on the local refining capacity and imports.
Midwest: ULSD typically trades at a discount here due to its closer proximity to the Gulf Coast resulting in lower transportation costs. It also benefits from increased refining capacity and cost savings on Bakken and Canadian Crude Grades.
Transport
Typically transported through batch pipelines, meaning different products are moved in sequential, non-mixing batches, requiring careful coordination and management of injection and withdrawal points to prevent product contamination. Major networks include the Colonial Pipeline from Houston to New Jersey and systems in the Midwest and Northeast United States that connect to these major trunk lines.
International Trade Flows
The USGC is one of the worlds largest exporters of diesel.
Europe is structurally short on diesel with their refining capacity so they rely on imports from the USGC
Latin America depends heavily on USGC exports for their trucking and industry.
The east coast (PADD 1) is short on reining capacity and relies heavily on shipments from the USGC (Colonial Pipeline) as well as imports from Europe.

Impact of Timing (Seasonality & Cycles)
Winter Heating vs Summer Freight Demand
The demand for ULSD exhibits distinct seasonal patterns. In regions of Europe and the Northeast United States where heating oil is still commonly used, it climbs in the winter. Because transport diesel and heating oil come from the same refinery streams, cold weather can raise prices. Additionally, when natural gas becomes limited, more houses and businesses are forced to burn heating oil, which increases pressure. Industry and freight are the main sources of demand in the summer. Consumer goods and tourism drive up trucking, construction projects rise, and farms require more fuel for their machinery. As supply chains experience peak activity, rail and maritime also move more. Due to its persistent summer draw, diesel consistently drives refinery profits and maintains prices that are lower than those of gasoline.
Source: eia.gov
Inventory Cycles
In the U.S., diesel inventories move in fairly regular cycles. Large volumes are stored in Gulf Coast tank farms near refineries, East Coast terminals linked to pipelines and barges, Midwest sites that serve farming and transport, and smaller, more remote facilities on the West Coast. Together these locations can hold hundreds of millions of barrels, though working levels are usually much lower.
Inventories typically build in spring and summer when refineries run hard to meet gasoline demand and produce diesel alongside it. They often draw down in fall and winter as heating oil use rises in the Northeast and freight demand peaks during harvest and the holiday season. Strikes, severe weather, or refinery outages can also swing stocks sharply.
These cycles have shown up time and again. Inventories climbed during the mid-2010s oil slump when demand was weak but refineries kept running, then fell to multi-decade lows in 2022 as exports surged and supply tightened.
Source: oilprice.com
Main Supply Disruptions
The main supply disruptions for diesel are much like those for other refined fuels, with most stemming from disruptions to crude oil supply.
Refinery outages: Planned maintenance or unexpected breakdowns cut diesel output and draw down inventories.
Hurricanes: In the U.S. Gulf Coast, storms can shut refineries, pipelines, and ports, causing sharp price spikes.
Strikes: Labor actions, especially in Europe, can halt refinery runs or fuel transport, tightening supply quickly.
Seasonal Diesel Blends: Summer vs Winter
Diesel changes with the seasons. In winter, refiners add lighter fuel or additives to prevent gelling, which slightly reduces power but keeps engines running in the cold. In summer, they switch back to regular diesel with higher energy content, with winter blends especially important in colder regions like Canada, the northern U.S., and Europe.
Source: centretank.com
Historical and Forward Perspectives
Diesel Fuel Timeline
1893: Rudolf Diesel invents the diesel engine.
1923: First direct-injection diesel engine
1945: Modern diesel fuel standards introduced post-WWII.
1978: Mercedes-Benz launches first turbodiesel passenger car.
1989: Dodge Ram gets the first pickup truck with a direct-injection diesel.
1993: U.S. starts limiting sulphur in diesel fuel.
2005: U.S. raises fuel quality standards for diesel.
2006: U.S. requires Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel (clean diesel) nationwide.
Historical Perspective
Diesel has powered industry, transport, and farming since the early 1900s. In the mid-2000s, the U.S. adopted Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel (15 ppm), cutting emissions and enabling cleaner engines. Demand has stayed steadier than gasoline because it is tied to freight, farming, construction, and heating. Globally, rules like IMO 2020 boosted diesel’s importance by shifting ships toward cleaner fuels.
Source: mansfield.energy
Forward Perspective
Diesel demand will stay strong in the near term, driven by trucking, shipping, farming, and industry. Europe will remain import-dependent, while the U.S. Gulf Coast and Asia serve as key export hubs. Over time, growth may slow with new policies, efficiency, and electrification, while renewable diesel and biodiesel grow as supplements. Even so, diesel will remain essential for heavy transport and global trade.
Source: researchgate.net
Regulatory Perspectives
Regulation has shaped the diesel market more than anything else. The mid-2000s shift to ultra-low sulphur diesel was a turning point, later extended to rail, marine, off-road, and heating oil. Europe and global shipping followed, making cleaner diesel the worldwide standard. Today the focus has moved from sulphur to carbon, with governments pushing lower-emission fuels and blending alternatives. Looking ahead, rules on diesel use in transport and shipping are likely to tighten further.

Upstream – Crude Producers
Grade: Traders market medium crudes (better diesel yields) to refiners that want distillate-heavy output.
Timing: Hedge production ahead of refinery maintenance or seasonal demand spikes.
Location: Sell into regions (USGC, Europe, Asia) with the highest diesel cracks.
Transport & Gathering (Crude Midstream)
Grade: Move crude grades that maximize diesel output to the right refineries.
Timing: Profit when pipeline space is scarce during high refinery runs or peak demand.
Location: Arbitrage inland vs coastal crude spreads (rail vs pipeline vs tanker).
Refining (Turning Crude into ULSD)
Grade: Choose crude slates that yield more diesel when cracks are strong.
Timing: Run higher or lower utilization around seasonal demand cycles and outages.
Location: Adjust production toward local or export markets depending on regional spreads.
Storage & Distribution (Products Midstream)
Grade: Send higher-sulphur fuel into heating oil, while keeping cleaner fuel for ULSD markets.
Timing: Store diesel when future prices are higher than today, and sell quickly when today’s price is better.
Location: Move extra Gulf Coast diesel by pipeline or barge to New York Harbor, where it usually sells at a higher price.
Wholesale & Retail Supply
Grade: Sell into ULSD or heating oil markets based on product quality and what customers need.
Timing: Take advantage of higher demand in winter for heating and in summer for freight.
Location: Deliver fuel to areas like truck stops or dealers where limited supply makes prices higher.
End Use (Consumers of ULSD)
Grade: Large fleets sometimes negotiate spec flexibility (ULSD vs blended fuel).
Timing: Hedge fuel costs ahead of seasonal price spikes (harvest, holiday freight, winter).
Location: Rail, trucking, and marine companies secure term contracts in the regions they operate to avoid paying spot premiums.

Lessons Learned
The major takeaway from this project for us, was to ensure that our trade accurately reflects the aspects we want it to. To ensure that our trades aren’t taking any unnecessary risks that aren’t being accounted for in our S&D model. Our original market call was a relatively basic trade thesis based on supply and demand characteristics, as well as some market sentiment that we thought was over priced in the market. Based on this we traded flat price and this left us particularly vulnerable to geopolitical issues that ended up creating a substantial uptick in the market. We have since learned to go further in depth with setting up our trade structure as to only take on the risk we have evaluated in our model and try to eliminate external risk factors as much as possible.
Another learning moment for us was to take our profit when the opportunity presented itself and also cut our loss as soon as factors outside of our control came into play. Our market call was initially proving to be successful, such that at one point our position was up over $1,000,000 in PL. This was very exciting for us, however instead of exiting our position we decided to hold a little longer. In the next couple days, Trump announced additional sanctions on Russian resources causing our market to completely flip and our PL began dropping. We held onto hope probably longer then we should’ve and instead of exiting our positions after our PL had dropped below $-1,000,000 we ended up waiting until being down over $-2.7M to cut our losses. This was a valuable learning lesson in the market, to let the winners win and exit out of those positions as soon as they begin to show signs of reverting and also cut losing positions quickly and cleanly.
Appendix
Price By PADD
Diesel prices vary across U.S. regions because each PADD has distinct refining capacity, demand patterns, environmental regulations, and logistics constraints. Some areas, like the East Coast, rely heavily on imports and use ULSD as heating oil, while others, such as the Gulf Coast, produce more fuel than they consume. These structural differences create persistent price spreads between regions.
PADD 1 – East Coast: The East Coast depends on pipeline shipments and imports from Europe, as local refining capacity is limited. Diesel prices are strongly influenced by winter heating demand, since ULSD also serves as heating oil. Seasonal draws and supply tightness often make PADD 1 the most expensive region.
PADD 2 – Midwest: Midwestern prices reflect agricultural cycles and transport activity. Demand spikes during planting and harvest seasons, while supply comes mainly from pipelines connected to Gulf Coast refineries. Seasonal swings in farm diesel usage and logistical bottlenecks drive volatility.
PADD 3 – Gulf Coast: The Gulf Coast is the nation’s refining center, exporting large volumes of ULSD to global markets. With abundant supply and low transport costs, diesel here is typically the cheapest in the U.S. Prices move mainly with export margins, crude costs, and hurricane-related disruptions.
PADD 4 – Rocky Mountains: This region has limited refining capacity and depends on pipeline inflows from the Gulf Coast. Transport costs and local heating demand push prices higher than PADD 3. Geographic isolation and smaller storage capacity also make PADD 4 more sensitive to short-term supply shifts.
PADD 5 – West Coast: The West Coast is an isolated market with strict fuel standards, particularly in California under CARB regulations. These environmental rules increase production costs and restrict blending options. Limited pipeline connectivity amplifies local shortages, keeping West Coast diesel prices the highest and most volatile in the country.
From these three charts, the spread and geographic price differences clearly widen over time. From 2007–2013, the five PADDs move closely together with a narrow spread and high correlation. In 2019–2025, the spread and price range increase noticeably across regions. What changed?
At the moment, we don’t have a definitive answer. Our guest speaker Nima pointed to regulatory factors and PADD 5’s isolation, where demand has outpaced local supply and pushed prices higher.
