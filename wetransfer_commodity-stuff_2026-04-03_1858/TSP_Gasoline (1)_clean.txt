About Your Commodity
Gasoline VS. RBOB Futures
Gasoline is a refined product made from crude oil. It’s a mixture of hydrocarbons, blended with ethanol and additives, and it’s the fuel that powers most cars on the road today. At the pump, we see different grades like regular, mid-grade, and premium. Those grades are determined by octane rating, which measures resistance to knocking in an engine. The gasoline you buy at the pump isn’t traded directly. Instead, the financial markets trade a standardized product called RBOB = Reformulated Blendstock for Oxygenate Blending. RBOB is basically unfinished gasoline: it has 87 octane, low sulfur, and no ethanol. Ethanol is added later at distribution terminals. In short, gasoline is the retail fuel, while RBOB is the wholesale benchmark, ticker symbol RB, that gets traded.
How does gasoline gets to the pump?
RBOB gets transported via pipelines to terminals. There, it gets blended with ethanol & additives. This mix is then transported to stations and stored in underground tanks. The different grades for gasoline are:  Regular : 87 octane  Midgrade : 89-90 octane  Premium : 91-93 octane
Gasoline Production
Gasoline is the second product of the refining process after propane/butane.
This gasoline is still not ready for consumption by law. (High emissions)
RBOB is either blended with ethanol on site (if it is close to the final destination), or it is then pumped to specialized blending facilities close to the consumption point (stations).
The reason for this is that ethanol can not be transported via pipelines because it would corrode them.
After blending, RBOB becomes RFG (Reformulated Gasoline) and is transported via tanker trucks to the stations.
Another type of gasoline is CBOB. The main difference between the two is that RBOB is harder to produce (lower Reid Vapor Pressure). While both are blended with ethanol, RBOB is used in producing RFG which meets stricter environmental requirements and used in areas with heavy smog.
Fun Fact: If a refinery receives 43 barrel of crude oil, it will output 46 barrels of refined products due to processing gain. Lower densities
Gasoline Supply
Because the USA is the largest producer and consumer of gasoline, we will mainly focus on the supply of gasoline in the US market.
The US consumed ~9m bb/day in 2023, 95% of which was used for road transport
To cover this huge demand, they use a mix of local production and imports
In 2024, Canadian crude accounted for 60% of total US crude oil imports. This figure represented almost 82% of total Canadian crude produced that year!
The majority of that Canadian crude was imported into the Gulf coast and Midwest refineries.
The Midwest and Gulf coast incidentally contain the top 10 refineries by production quantities in the US along with the West Coast. (PADD 2,3, & 5)
Each one of these refineries produces between 360 and 630 thousand barrels of gasoline per day. Fun Fact: PADD stands for US Petroleum Administration for Defense Districts - Started in WW2 and used to monitor and manage fuel and diesel supply & usage. Also used in the Korea war. Now they are mainly used to gather data for the EIA
How does RBOB Futures trade?
Contracts are listed for every calendar month, and while they are technically physically deliverable, most traders close or roll their positions before delivery. This setup allows for both hedging and speculation without the need to actually handle gasoline.
What traders watch Seasonality (Summer Driving Season): Demand accelerates, inventories are often low, and EPA requires lower-volatility summer blends, which are more expensive to produce. volatility is the ease of evaporation
Inventory surprises: Weekly EIA reports (Wednesdays 9:30 a.m. CT) on gasoline inventories, especially in PADD 1b (Central Atlantic), are closely watched. Inventories here indicate how much gasoline could be delivered into RBOB contracts. Deviations from expectations can quickly move prices.
Disruptions
Weather and operational events (hurricanes, flooding, refinery outages, pipeline issues) threaten supply. Traders watch these closely, as even the threat of production loss can lift RBOB futures.
Most U.S. refining capacity is concentrated on the Gulf Coast (Texas and Louisiana). This makes gasoline production vulnerable to regional shocks.
Hurricane season (June–November): Overlap with summer driving season and low inventories. Major storms (Harvey 2017, Ida 2021) forced multiple refineries offline, cutting millions of barrels of capacity.
The Colonial Pipeline cyberattack (2021) shut flows to the East Coast for 5 days, causing shortages and a surge in New York Harbor prices. Terminals and storage hubs are also critical chokepoints.
Recovery is slow. After a disruption, supply chains take weeks to normalize as pipelines refill and storage tanks rebuild. Low stocks amplify price impacts.
Different gasoline grades (RBOB vs. CBOB) limit flexibility to reroute supply, making disruptions more persistent than in crude markets.
Traders price in both the immediate loss of supply and the lag in recovery. RBOB futures often react sharply, with calendar spreads and crack spreads used to express views on disruption risk.

What are inventories and why they matter:
Supply and demand are in a continuous tug-of-war game and the winner alternates between the two. Inventory is the balancing factor between them that keeps the rope stable.
When demand exceeds supply, the excess gasoline is put into storage (inventory build up).
When the opposite occurs, the shortfall is covered by inventories (draw down)
These build ups/draw downs are used by analysts as indicators of price direction, market demand, refinery outages etc…
Relationship between inventory levels and futures curve
Inventories have a higher impact on short-term and spot prices of gasoline, especially relating to inventory suprises
Backwardated markets provide the incentive for companies to draw down inventories and sell RBOB in the spot market over storing it for later sales
Markets in contango give the incentive to store gasoline and sell in the futures market for higher prices.

Refinery Output: Factors Influencing Output
Utilization Rate: the % of total operable capacity that is being used
Planned (post winter & pre summer maintenance) & unplanned outages. (Inventories and Utilization rates are used by analysts to catch outages)
Market signals: If crack spread is high, utilization will remain higher even if inventory levels are high. Refineries are a business, and will optimize profits.
Crude supply: High supply/inventories support refinery operations if demand and market conditions are favorable. If crude levels drop, while RBOB demand remains elevated, then utilization could drop due to a bottleneck in supply.
Yields: the ratios of refined products produced (%)
Seasonality and RVP: Summer gasoline has lower RVP and is harder/more expensive to make
Crack spread: Refineries can tweak product yield to optimize profits. This depends on refinery complexity and input qualities. (If it has a coker or not, and if its heavy vs light crude)
Refineries usually have short-term drop in utilization/gasoline production in sep for a few reasons - slow down in demand, focus on distillate production instead

Balancing Factor
Inventories:
Inventory drawdown generally indicates demand is outpacing supply, which leads to higher spot prices
Inventory surplus usually has the opposite effect (short term graph here)
Seasonality: Inventory changes usually follow a seasonal trend.
Inventory in Summer is used up to meet demand, built back up in Winter
When the switch to higher RVP gasoline occurs, inventories drop as tanks are turned around to avoid mixing summer and winter gasoline. (Leftovers are sold quickly on sale, or blended later on with winter gasoline / butane)
Build up in October of Winter gasoline, and draw down in November due to exports

Lessons Learned
What have you learned from this project? Researching RBOB market dynamics and attempting to capitalize on a market view has been a very challenging but rewarding experience. We learned a lot about the hidden layers and processes that we take for granted every day (go to station and pump gas) and noticed that the deeper we looked, the further the bottom of the hole became. In fact, it is a bottomless pit.
Trading any commodity is not easy, and trying to form a profitable market view using publicly available information is very difficult. Privileged information flow gives traders an edge in reading and capitalizing on market signals.
We also learned 2 fundamental trading lessons. One of them is to keep track of your positions, and make note of when trading terminates for each contract you enter. Another is being very careful when entering your trades to enter the correct contract, and commodity.
